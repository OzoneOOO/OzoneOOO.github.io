# Retirement Intelligence: City Research Guide

**Purpose:** This document is the definitive instruction manual for AI models (Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT) tasked with researching and drafting city profiles for the Retirement Intelligence project. 

It defines the exact data points required to populate the two primary HTML files (`retirement_politics.html` and `retirement_weather.html`) and dictates the analytical depth expected for each section.

**Prerequisite:** Before drafting any content, the model MUST read and internalize the "Intelligent Advisor" tone guidelines defined in `AGENTS.md`. The output must be objective, quantified, strictly analytical, and applied explicitly to the context of a retiree.

---

## Part 1: Politics, Economy & Society (`retirement_politics.html`)

For each of the 8 sections below, the AI must gather hard data and synthesize it to explain the *structural mechanisms* at play. 

### 1. Governance & Electoral Structure
*   **Data Required:** Form of municipal government (e.g., strong mayor, council-manager). Current mayor (name, party, professional background, electoral margin, term limits). City council structural composition (e.g., district vs. at-large) and ideological makeup.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Explain the actual power dynamic. Is the mayor politically vulnerable or exceptionally popular? Are they aligned or in conflict with the city council and/or the state government? Focus on functional stability.

### 2. Fiscal Health & Tax Structure
*   **Data Required (Municipal):** Total operating budget, size of deficit/surplus, primary municipal revenue sources (e.g., property tax, local earnings tax, sales tax). Credit rating if noteworthy.
*   **Data Required (Retiree Tax Burden):** State and local taxation *as it applies to retirees*. Specifically: Is Social Security taxed? Are public/private pensions exempt? What is the standard property tax rate or median bill? Are there senior property tax exemptions?
*   **Analytical Depth:** Separate the city's fiscal health from the individual's tax burden. A city might be broke while offering low taxes, or fiscally sound due to punishing taxes. Identify structural fiscal cliffs (e.g., expiring pandemic funds, declining commercial property assessments).

### 3. Public Safety & Disorder Philosophy
*   **Data Required:** Hard, recent crime statistics (e.g., exact homicide numbers, percentage drops/increases in violent and property crime, clearance rates). Homeless Point-in-Time counts if relevant.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Do not aggregate crime generally; specify violent vs. property crime. Note geographic concentration (does crime affect the whole city or just specific corridors?). Objectively state the administration's philosophical approach to homelessness and disorder (e.g., enforcement-heavy vs. shelter-first) without moralizing.

### 4. Housing & Development Policy
*   **Data Required:** Median single-family home prices and typical rent for a 1-2 bedroom apartment. Housing inventory levels (months of supply) and recent appreciation/depreciation trends.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Name the mechanism driving the prices. Is the city constrained by geography (oceans/mountains), strict exclusionary zoning, population surges, or loss of housing stock? Assess the market accessibility for a retiree arriving with equity versus one relying strictly on fixed income.

### 5. Economic Trajectory
*   **Data Required:** Major industries, anchor employers, and recent job growth/loss figures. Broad demographic shifts (is the metro gaining or losing population?).
*   **Analytical Depth:** Explain why the local economy matters to a retiree who doesn't need a job. Connect the economic trajectory to the municipal tax base, the vibrancy of local retail/restaurants, and the quality of city services. 

### 6. Transit & Infrastructure Investment
*   **Data Required:** Primary transit modes (rail, bus, BRT). Major ongoing or funded infrastructure projects. Airport connectivity (direct flights to major hubs).
*   **Analytical Depth:** Answer the core question: *Can a retiree live here comfortably without a car?* Differentiate between neighborhoods where car-free living is possible versus the broader metro reality.

### 7. Social Environment
*   **Data Required:** Demographic/racial breakdown. Presence of major cultural/academic/medical institutions. LGBTQ+ legal protections (state vs. local) and Municipal Equality Index (HRC) scores.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Describe the cultural "feel" of the city. Identify if the city's social norms directly conflict with the surrounding state's laws (e.g., a progressive city in a socially conservative state). Evaluate the accessibility and quality of the healthcare network.

### 8. Political Stability & Trajectory
*   **Data Required:** Upcoming major elections. Documented instances of state preemption or intervention in city affairs. 
*   **Analytical Depth:** What is the biggest risk to the status quo over a 10-20 year retirement horizon? Synthesize the internal political volatility alongside external threats (e.g., a hostile state legislature, severe economic dependence on a single industry).

## Part 2: Climate & Environment (`retirement_weather.html`)

Weather analysis must go beyond "nice" or "cold." It must assess physical risk, infrastructure preparation, and health consequences for an aging population.

### 1. Climate Overview & Baseline
*   **Data Required:** Köppen climate classification. Latitude and elevation. Key geographic modifiers (valleys, lakes, oceans, mountains). Average annual temperature range, total precipitation, and annual snowfall.

### 2. Winter — Severity, Variability, and Cold Risk
*   **Data Required:** Average January highs/lows. Record lows. Typical snow vs. freezing rain/ice accumulation.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Focus on the *character* of the winter. Is the primary risk extreme cold (cardiovascular stress), massive snow dumps, or freezing rain (fall hazards and power outages on ice)? How capable is the local infrastructure at clearing it?

### 3. Summer — Heat, Humidity, and Health
*   **Data Required:** Average July/August highs and typical peak Heat Index/Humidex values.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Assess the urban heat island effect and nighttime cooling (or lack thereof). Note the prevalence of residential air conditioning (e.g., is the housing stock prepared for the heat?). Detail the health risks of high heat/humidity for cardiovascular or respiratory patients.

### 4. Sunshine & SAD Risk
*   **Data Required:** Annual sunshine hours. Days of sunshine per year. Winter daylight hours (especially around the solstice).
*   **Analytical Depth:** Evaluate the risk of Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD). Is the winter gray chronic and relentless (marine overcast), or are there bright, clear, cold days? Differentiate between light deprivation caused by extreme latitude (short days) vs. atmospheric conditions (cloud cover).

### 5. Extreme Weather & Natural Hazards
*   **Data Required:** Historical frequency and severity of earthquakes, tornadoes, hurricanes, landslides, and flash floods.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Do not treat a 500-year earthquake risk the same as an annual tornado season. Quantify the risks realistically for a 20-30 year retirement window. 

### 6. Air Quality
*   **Data Required:** EPA/WHO air quality rankings. Primary pollution sources.
*   **Analytical Depth:** Identify the mechanism. Is it summer ground-level ozone? Winter valley inversions trapping wood smoke? A growing seasonal threat from regional wildfire smoke? Specify the time of year when air quality is dangerous.

### 7. Water — Supply, Drought, and Infrastructure
*   **Data Required:** Primary source of drinking water (e.g., specific aquifer, reservoir, river, or glacier). 
*   **Analytical Depth:** Evaluate the resilience of the system. Is it a single-source system vulnerable to a single wildfire or earthquake? Is it dependent on shrinking snowpack or a declining aquifer?

### 8. How Residents Adapt
*   **Data Required:** Behavioral, infrastructural, and cultural adaptations.
*   **Analytical Depth:** How do locals actually live here? (e.g., "Nobody goes outside from noon to 4 PM in August," "Everyone uses SAD lamps and takes Vitamin D," "Homes have basements for tornadoes").

### 9. Climate Change Trajectory
*   **Data Required:** Specific, localized scientific projections for mid-century (e.g., "days over 95°F will increase from 7 to 38," "sea-level rise will inundate X neighborhoods").
*   **Analytical Depth:** What is the trajectory for the next 20-30 years? Is the city's government actively migrating infrastructure, or are they ignoring the risk?

### 10. Comparisons to Baselines
*   **Analytical Depth:** The model must explicitly compare the city's weather to four established baseline profiles:
    1.  **San Diego, CA:** The ideal baseline (mild, sunny, low variability, low hazard).
    2.  **Seattle, WA:** The mild but dark baseline (marine overcast, low extreme heat/cold, high SAD risk).
    3.  **Columbia, MO:** The continental extremes baseline (hot/humid summers, cold/icy winters, tornado risk).
    4.  **Richland, WA:** The arid extremes baseline (hot dry summers, cold bright winters, no severe storms). 
    *The comparison should highlight exact trade-offs (e.g., "City X offers San Diego's winters but requires trading it for Columbia's humid summers").*
