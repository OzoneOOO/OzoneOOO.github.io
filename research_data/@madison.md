# Madison, WI — Research Notes
**Date gathered:** 2026-04-25  
**Coordinates:** 43.07°N, 89.43°W

## POLITICS DIMENSIONS

### 1. Governance Form
- Strong-mayor / city council structure
- Mayor serves as chief executive: proposes budget, appoints department heads, oversees daily operations
- Common Council: 20 alderpersons elected from 20 single-member districts; 2-year terms
- Current mayor: Satya Rhodes-Conway (progressive Democrat), in office since 2019; re-elected 2023
- April 7, 2026 general election for common council seats

### 2. Fiscal Health
- Moody's AAA bond rating (highest possible); city borrows at lowest available interest rates
- 2026 Executive Capital Budget: $73.5M lower in GO borrowing than agency requests
- City described as "financially healthy" with local economy outpacing rest of Wisconsin
- Revenue strengthened by elevated interest rates boosting investment income
- Identified fiscal risk: federal Medicaid/safety-net cuts will increase community need and downstream city costs
- 2025 mid-year budget required balancing action (not structural crisis; reflects one-time pressures)

### 3. Retiree Tax Burden
- Wisconsin income tax: 3.5%–7.65% on a graduated scale
- Social Security: **not taxed** by Wisconsin
- Retirement income subtraction: up to **$24,000** for single filers age 67+ (up to $48,000 for qualifying joint filers) on qualified retirement income
- Property tax: ~1.96% effective rate; median annual Dane County property tax ~$6,494 — highest in the state
- November 2024: Madison Metropolitan School District passed $100M recurring operating + $507M capital referendum; adds ~$327/yr to average homeowner's bill starting December 2025
- Senior property tax assistance programs available through Dane County
- Tax Foundation ranks Wisconsin mid-tier nationally on overall tax burden

### 4. Crime
- 2024 total crime rate: 2,120.9 per 100,000 (slightly above national rate; 48% above Wisconsin average)
- Violent crime rate: 256 per 100,000; chance of violent victimization 1-in-391
- Property crime: chance 1-in-54
- 2024 vs. 2023: overall crime fell 20%; homicides 6 (down from 10); aggravated battery down; robberies lowest in 12+ years
- Motor vehicle theft increased: 742 in 2024 vs. 592 in 2023 (mirrors national trend)
- 2025 YTD: 16% further reduction vs. 3-year average (2022–2024) across major crime categories
- Police Chief John Patterson cited "significant and impressive reductions"

### 5. Housing Policy & Market
- Mayor's "Housing Forward" initiative: target 15,000 new units by 2030; three packages passed by Common Council in 2025
- Zoning overhaul allows duplexes/twin homes on most residentially zoned lots (previously banned in many neighborhoods)
- "Missing middle" housing (duplexes, coach houses, cottage courts) now permitted more broadly
- Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) overlay district created 2023 along high-frequency transit corridors
- Removed minimum private backyard space requirement for residential development
- **Median home price (Feb 2026):** $420K (down 2.5% YoY); state median ~$297K for context
- Average rent: $1,814/month (up ~1.9% YoY); median rent across unit types ~$1,578
- 1BR avg: $1,565 | 2BR avg: $1,941 | Studio avg: $1,274
- Rents up 47% since 2020; competitive seller's market (median 12 days to pending)

### 6. Economic Trajectory
- **University of Wisconsin–Madison:** largest employer; $30.8B annual statewide economic impact; 232,000 jobs attributed
- **Wisconsin state government:** second-largest employer anchor
- **Epic Systems:** largest private-sector employer (healthcare IT); headquartered in nearby Verona
- Other significant private employers: American Family Insurance, Sub-Zero, Trek Bicycle, Lands' End, American Girl
- Tech sector: Google (100+ employees), Microsoft (Gray Systems Lab), Zendesk (300+ employees, Midwest hub)
- Tech labor market grew 47% from 2014–2019
- Unemployment rate: 2.4% (December 2025) vs. Wisconsin state 3.1% (August 2025)
- Economy described as consistently outpacing rest of Wisconsin

### 7. Public Transit & Walkability
- Madison Metro Transit operates bus network; BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) corridors planned/in development
- **City-wide Walk Score: 50** ("somewhat walkable") — outer neighborhoods car-dependent
- **Downtown / Isthmus Walk Score: 92–95** ("walker's paradise")
- Most walkable neighborhoods: Downtown, State-Langdon, Marquette (all on isthmus)
- **Platinum-level Bicycle Friendly Community** (League of American Bicyclists)
- PeopleForBikes 2025 City Ratings: ranked among top US cities for cycling
- Recent path extensions: Autumn Ridge Path, Garver Path connecting to downtown
- Vision Zero Safe Streets 2025 projects: high-visibility crosswalks, green bike markings, speed reductions in high-injury zones
- City-wide walking trips ~175,000/day (up from ~150,000 in 2019)
- Draft pedestrian plan targets 85% walkability to schools, grocery, transit (currently 55–63%)
- Pedestrian comfort challenges: unsafe driver behavior, lack of separation from traffic

### 8. LGBTQ+ Environment & Social Climate
- **2025 HRC Municipal Equality Index: 122/122 — only city in the US to achieve maximum possible score**
- 2023 score: 100/100 (methodology change expanded to 49 criteria, max score became 122)
- Designated 2025 MEI "All-Star City" (high score despite lacking state-level nondiscrimination laws)
- Deep progressive political culture; city has long been considered one of the most LGBTQ-affirming cities in the Midwest
- Note: local journalism (Tone Madison) documents that LGBTQ infrastructure most accessible to cisgender, white, higher-income residents — equity gaps exist within the community
- University of Wisconsin–Madison campus adds substantial LGBTQ institutional presence

### 9. Political Stability (20–30 Year Horizon)
- City has voted Democratic in every presidential election for 50+ years
- Progressive Dane (local third party founded 1992) reflects depth of left political culture
- Tradition of "Fighting Bob" La Follette progressivism; *The Progressive* magazine still published in Madison
- City-level governance extremely stable; progressive majority reliably elected to council
- **Key tension:** Republican-controlled Wisconsin state legislature (since Walker era 2011) has historically preempted or constrained Madison city policies (minimum wage, paid leave, etc.)
- State-city preemption dynamic is an ongoing structural vulnerability for progressive local governance
- Governor Tony Evers (Democrat) re-elected 2022 provides some executive check on legislature
- Long-term risk: continued Republican state legislature gerrymandering could constrain city autonomy on key issues
- Democratic presidential margin in Dane County consistently 70%+

## WEATHER DIMENSIONS

### General Climate
- **Köppen classification:** Dfa (humid continental, hot summer) — some sources classify as Dfb (warm summer) near the boundary
- Four highly distinct seasons; severe winters with regular sub-zero temperatures
- No dry season; precipitation distributed year-round with slight summer peak
- Isthmus location between Lake Mendota and Lake Monona creates mild lake effect but does not moderate winters significantly

### Precipitation
- Annual precipitation: ~37 inches (936 mm)
- Annual snowfall: ~40–45 inches typical; heavy lake-effect enhancement possible
- May is wettest month; February driest
- Rain distributed throughout year; summer months slightly wetter

### Extreme Weather
- **Tornadoes:** avg 0.7 tornado days/year; Dane County within the southern Wisconsin tornado belt; F1 tornado hit western Madison directly in a notable outbreak
- **Winter storms:** severe; regular Arctic outbreaks; January 2026 recorded "coldest air in several years"; lake ice-cover on Mendota and Monona most winters
- **Flooding:** ~16% of Madison buildings at flood risk; several downtown intersections historically prone to flash flooding; increased extreme precipitation events projected
- **Wildfire:** ~81% of buildings have some wildfire risk but risk level rated "relatively low" — mainly from regional smoke transport

### Air Quality
- Annual average AQI: ~37–40 (Good range)
- 2025: 130 good air quality days (72% of monitored days); 1 unhealthy day
- Grade A; ranks better than ~60% of US cities
- Primary concern: PM2.5 from traffic and periodic wildfire smoke transport
- No significant industrial air quality burden

### Water Access & Quality
- **Exceptional water access:** city built on isthmus between **Lake Mendota** (9,842 acres, 82 ft deep) and **Lake Monona** (3,274 acres, 64 ft deep); additional Yahara chain lakes (Wingra, Waubesa, Kegonsa) nearby
- Drinking water: 22 wells, 30 reservoirs, 916 miles of pipes; meets all federal/state standards
- Water quality concern: **PFAS contamination** — low levels detected in half of Madison's wells (below regulatory limits); Lake Monona has elevated PFOS; fish consumption advisories issued for Lake Monona, Waubesa, Kegonsa
- Lake Mendota and Monona received "fair" rating for phosphorus and offshore clarity in 2024
- 2024 recorded highest rate of algae blooms since monitoring began; increased runoff from wetter conditions
- Drinking water quality considered safe; recreational water quality has measurable degradation trend

### Climate Change Projections
- By 2050: ~38 days/year over 90°F (from ~7 days circa 1990)
- Hot nights (above 70°F) projected to quadruple by 2050
- Annual precipitation projected to increase modestly (~34.9" from ~32.8")
- Proportion of precipitation falling as intense downpours projected to increase (to 46% from 42%)
- Flood risk: ~16% of buildings
- Wildfire risk: listed as low intensity but widespread (regional smoke transport)
- Madison has been discussed publicly as a potential **"climate haven"** due to Great Lakes water access, moderate heat relative to Sun Belt, and low drought risk — though this framing is contested
- The Crawfish watershed (which contains Madison) experienced drought conditions in 33% of weeks since 2000
- Wisconsin projected to warm significantly; state may resemble current southern states by mid-century

## NOTES FOR SECTION 10 (Weather Comparisons)
Baseline cities: San Diego CA, Seattle WA, Columbia MO, Richland WA

- Madison winters significantly colder than all four baseline cities
- January sunshine (3.3 hrs/day) far below San Diego (~7 hrs), below Seattle (~2–3 hrs), roughly comparable to Columbia MO (~4 hrs)
- Summer heat comparable to Columbia MO and Richland WA; milder than Richland summers
- Water access dramatically superior to Columbia MO and Richland WA; comparable to Seattle (saltwater vs. freshwater); San Diego has ocean
- Annual sunshine (~2,773 hrs) comparable to Seattle (~2,170 hrs) but below San Diego (~3,300 hrs), Richland (~2,900 hrs), Columbia MO (~2,600 hrs)
- Air quality notably better than most baseline cities
- Climate haven framing: plausible for heat/drought/wildfire resilience relative to Sun Belt; not relevant for cold/dark winters
