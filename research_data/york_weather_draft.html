York carries a Köppen Cfa classification (humid subtropical), defined by hot, humid summers and cool-to-cold winters with no dry season. The city lies in south-central Pennsylvania within the Piedmont region at an elevation of roughly 400 feet, well inland from any large body of water.

Without significant marine moderation, York's climate follows inland continental patterns: a wide annual temperature range, year-round precipitation, and a clear four-season profile. Codorus Creek runs through the city center, and the Susquehanna River lies approximately 15 miles to the west, but neither produces meaningful thermal buffering at the city scale.

Compared with the U.S. average climate, York is slightly warmer in summer, slightly cooler in winter, and substantially wetter year-round. The Cfa classification is shared with much of the U.S. mid-Atlantic and Southeast and is distinct from the Mediterranean (Csa/Csb) climate type, which features dry summers.

July is the warmest month, with average highs of 85–87°F and average lows of 63–64°F. August stays warm at roughly 84°F highs and 62°F lows, while June averages 82°F highs and 61°F lows. These values place York summers slightly above the U.S. average for daily highs in the peak months.

Summer humidity is a defining feature of the Cfa climate, with dew points commonly in the upper 60s to low 70s°F during July and August, which substantially raises the heat index above the dry-bulb temperature. Convective afternoon and evening thunderstorms are common, contributing both to peak-hour cooling and to flood risk.

The city currently averages roughly 12 days per year above 90°F, meaningfully fewer than continental interior cities further south but more than coastal Pacific climates. Heat humidity combinations during the warmest weeks routinely produce conditions that exceed comfort thresholds for outdoor exertion.

January is the coldest month, with average highs of 37–40°F and average lows of 21–23°F. February averages 43°F highs and 25°F lows, while December sees 42°F highs and 25°F lows. Winter overnight lows routinely fall below freezing through December, January, and February.

By mid-March, daily highs rise into the low 50s°F and overnight lows climb into the low 30s°F, marking the transition out of the deepest winter. November transitions from autumn into early winter with highs near 54°F and lows near 34°F.

York's winters are colder than the U.S. average for major metropolitan areas and well below any Mediterranean climate benchmark. Multiple consecutive months feature daily mean temperatures below the 50°F threshold often used as a marker for "mild winter" climates, and freeze-thaw cycles are routine.

Annual precipitation totals approximately 45 inches, above the U.S. national average of about 30 inches. Precipitation falls year-round with no pronounced dry season — a defining characteristic of the Cfa climate type — and September is typically the wettest month, in part because of tropical-system remnants.

Annual snowfall averages approximately 27 inches, slightly above the U.S. average of about 28 inches and well above southern Cfa regions. Snow events are typically distributed across December through March, with occasional significant storms producing larger single-event totals.

Summer precipitation is dominated by convective thunderstorms, which can deliver high-intensity rainfall in short windows. Winter storms produce a mix of snow, sleet, and freezing rain depending on temperature profiles, contributing to ice-related hazards for travel and infrastructure.

York averages approximately 2,527–2,609 sunshine hours per year, with roughly 201 sunny days annually. This is somewhat above the U.S. average annual sunshine total of about 2,500 hours but well below the levels typical of arid Western or Mediterranean climates, which often exceed 3,000 hours.

Daily sunshine peaks in July at around 11.0 hours per day and reaches secondary highs of 9.5 hours per day in June and August. May (8.5) and April (7.5) are also strong sunshine months as the warm season builds.

Winter sunshine drops substantially: December averages just 4.1 hours per day, January 5.4 hours, February 5.5 hours, and November 5.0 hours. These winter values reflect both shorter days and frequent cloud cover from mid-latitude storm systems crossing the region, putting winter solar irradiance well below the levels associated with sun-reliable climates.

Flooding represents the dominant extreme-weather hazard. Codorus Creek runs through the city center, and York County sits within the Susquehanna River Basin — one of the most flood-prone watersheds in the United States. The Susquehanna mainstem has flooded 14 times since 1810, roughly every 15 years on average, and the basin averages approximately $150 million in annual flood damage.

The benchmark event remains Hurricane Agnes in 1972, which dropped 13.5 inches of rain in 24 hours and caused Codorus Creek to split the city in two. Flash flooding regularly affects multiple downtown intersections during heavy convective storms, and ice jam flooding during winter thaw events represents an additional seasonal hazard.

Tornado risk exists at moderate mid-Atlantic levels, lower in frequency than continental Midwest tornado-alley regions but non-trivial during severe convective episodes. Heat extremes are an emerging hazard: York has been identified as having the highest projected heat risk among Pennsylvania cities, with current averages of about 12 days per year over 90°F projected to rise to 30 or more days by 2050.

York's Air Quality Index typically falls in the moderate range, with common readings around 50–70 — within the EPA's "Moderate" category, where unusually sensitive individuals may experience respiratory effects. The city does not rank among Pennsylvania's cleaner-air markets.

Manufacturing concentration in York County contributes to particulate (PM2.5) burden, with industrial emissions joining vehicle emissions and regional transport from the broader mid-Atlantic airshed. The city records more unhealthy air days per year than cleaner U.S. metro benchmarks, particularly during summer ozone episodes.

Wildfire smoke transport from Canadian or western U.S. fires can episodically degrade air quality further during peak fire seasons, although direct local wildfire risk is low. Overall air quality is acceptable for most residents but represents a meaningful negative differential against the cleanest U.S. metro areas.

York Water Company supplies drinking water drawn primarily from Codorus Creek, supported by two reservoirs — Lake Williams and Lake Redman — with a combined capacity of approximately 2.23 billion gallons. A Susquehanna River pumping station with up to 12 million gallons per day capacity, fed via a 15-mile pipeline, provides supplemental supply. Drinking water meets all federal and state standards.

Recreational water access is limited. Codorus Creek flows through the city center but is a small, channelized urban stream offering minimal recreational amenity. There is no major lake, bay, or ocean within the city or immediate metro area, and the Susquehanna River roughly 15 miles west provides no waterfront amenity for city residents on a daily basis.

Codorus Creek's recreational water quality has historically been degraded by agricultural and urban runoff in the watershed. For a retiree prioritizing meaningful daily water access — coastline, large lake, or navigable river — York's water-amenity profile is weak by national standards.

Pennsylvania is projected to warm by approximately 5.9 to 7°F by 2050 under current emissions trajectories. York specifically has been identified as having the highest heat risk among Pennsylvania cities, with the current 12 days per year above 90°F projected to rise to 30 or more days per year by 2050 — a roughly 2.5x increase in extreme heat days.

Intense precipitation events are projected to increase by approximately 8% by 2050, amplifying flood risk in the Susquehanna watershed where storm intensification interacts with already flood-prone topography. Codorus Creek's confined urban channel makes flash-flood escalation a particular concern for the city center.

Direct wildfire risk remains low, though smoke transport events from distant fires are likely to continue and may intensify with broader regional warming. The dominant climate-change exposures for York are heat amplification and flood intensification rather than drought or wildfire.

Against the four baseline cities, York's winters are milder than Columbia MO and Richland WA, comparable to or slightly colder than Seattle, and much colder than San Diego. January average highs of 37–40°F sit well above the deeper continental winters of the inland baselines but below San Diego's mild Mediterranean winter regime.

Summer climate runs hotter and more humid than San Diego and Seattle, comparable to Columbia MO in temperature and humidity, and cooler than Richland WA's hottest peak summer days, although Richland's dry-heat profile differs sharply from York's humid heat. January sunshine of approximately 5.4 hours per day exceeds Seattle, falls below San Diego, and is similar to Columbia MO and Richland WA.

Annual sunshine totals near 2,600 hours sit well below San Diego (~3,300 hours) and Richland WA (~2,900 hours), above Seattle (~2,170 hours), and broadly similar to Columbia MO. Water access is a significant weakness: York has no ocean, no bay, and no large lake, making it materially inferior to San Diego and Seattle on water amenity, while Codorus Creek offers minimal recreational value compared with Columbia MO's river access or Richland WA's Columbia River frontage.

Flood risk is notably higher than at any of the four baseline cities, driven by Codorus Creek and the broader Susquehanna basin. Air quality is moderate — better than some heavily industrial markets but not as clean as the Pacific baseline cities of San Diego and Seattle.
