## POLITICS

### 1. Governance & Electoral Structure
- "Council-Mayor" form (not council-manager); established 2004, effective 2005
- Mayor: **Danny Avula** (D), sworn in January 1, 2025; replacing Levar Stoney; 4-year term, max 2 consecutive terms
- Mayor oversees Chief Administrative Officer (CAO); Odie Donald II appointed as new CAO June 2025
- 9-member council elected from 9 geographic districts; 4-year terms (changed from 2-year in 2009)
- Council President: Cynthia I. Newbille (2025–2026); Vice President: Katherine Jordan
- Virginia is moderately progressive: first Southern state to ban LGBTQ discrimination (2020); Democratic-controlled legislature; Gov. Youngkin (R) term ends Jan 2026
- City-state alignment: City is solidly Democratic; has operated in friction with Youngkin but Democratic legislature provided a buffer; next governor's politics matter

### 2. Fiscal Health & Tax Structure
- **FY2026 budget (adopted):** $3.0 billion (operating + 5-year capital improvement plan)
- **FY2027 proposed:** $3.4 billion (Avula presented March 2026)
- Property tax rate: $1.20 per $100 assessed value (maintained through 2026; council rejected push to lower rates)
- Real estate tax revenue: $494M (2025) → $523M projected (2026) — growing tax base
- No structural deficit reported; budget appears balanced at time of research
- **Virginia retiree tax burden:**
  - Social Security: NOT taxed by Virginia
  - Pensions/IRAs/401k: taxable as ordinary income; Virginia progressive rates 2%–5.75%
  - Age deduction: up to $12,000 for age 65+; phases out $1-for-$1 above $50K AGI (single) or $75K (married)
  - Effective property tax: ~0.71% statewide; Richmond rate $1.20/$100 assessed = 1.2% of assessed value
  - No local earnings tax; no estate/inheritance tax
  - Senior property tax relief programs available (locality-specific)

### 3. Public Safety
- **2025 crime:**
  - Homicides: 54 (same as 2024; 2024 was lowest since 2018); 10-year average ~60/year
  - Violent crime: up 8% from 2024 (which had fallen below 1,000 incidents — historically low)
  - Commercial robberies: +32%; carjackings: -39%; residential robberies: -40%; major crimes overall -12%
- **Rates:** violent crime ~10.8 per 1,000 (1,080 per 100,000); property crime 42.3 per 1,000
- Context: Richmond violent crime is among the highest in Virginia statewide
- Geographic concentration: crime concentrated in specific corridors; East End, parts of South Side
- 2024 saw landmark low violent crime; 2025 showed partial reversal in some categories

### 4. Housing & Development
- Median home price: ~$400K (Feb 2026), up 5.3% YoY
- Median rent (all types): ~$1,600/month (March 2026), up 3.59% YoY; 16% below national average
- Homes selling after 30 days on market (vs 18 days a year prior — market cooling slightly)
- Average home value up 1.3% over past year; broadly stable/moderate appreciation
- Market: moderately competitive; no geographic hard constraint like coastal or mountain cities
- Inventory: Improving from tight highs; more balanced than coastal metros
- Mechanism: demand driven by finance/tech/healthcare employment base, in-migration from DC/Northern VA corridor; no hard geographic ceiling

### 5. Economic Trajectory
- MSA total employment: 734,700 (June 2025)
- Unemployment: 3.1% (city), 2.7% (MSA)
- Top sectors: Government (119,800 jobs), Educational & Health Services (117,000), Finance/Insurance (~117,300), Leisure & Hospitality (69,000)
- Top employers: Capital One (~14,000), VCU Health (~13,500), HCA Virginia (~11,200), Bon Secours (~8,500)

- Major manufacturing: LEGO manufacturing/distribution facility; DuPont largest global facility

- Job growth: "little changed" overall vs prior year; national pace 1.1%; tech sector projects 16% growth

### 6. Transit & Infrastructure
- **GRTC bus:** 157+ vehicles (diesel + CNG), 45 routes, 5AM–1AM daily, seven days/week
- **GRTC Pulse BRT:** 7.6 miles, Broad and Main Streets corridor from Rocketts Landing to Willow Lawn; Bronze BRT standard (ITDP); >6,100 daily riders; operating at max capacity; new buses entering service 2025
- Zero-fare program: extended through end of FY2026 via $6.8M state funding
- **Walk Score:** 84 ("very walkable") for city core
- Car-free realistic: core neighborhoods (The Fan, Museum District, Carytown, Downtown, Church Hill) support car-light/car-free living; broader metro is car-dependent
- **Airport:** Richmond International Airport (RIC) — direct flights to major East Coast hubs; connecting flights for cross-country and international
- No light rail; BRT is the primary rapid transit spine

### 7. Social Environment
- **HRC MEI score:** 100/100 (2024) — perfect score
- **Virginia state LGBTQ law:** First Southern state to ban discrimination in employment, housing, public accommodations, and credit (effective July 1, 2020); hate crime law includes sexual orientation and gender identity
- Active tension with Youngkin's administration: transgender athlete ban (Feb 2025); school policy conflicts; conversion therapy enforcement dispute
- Richmond city: did not adopt Youngkin's school gender policies; affirmed existing local protections
- **Demographics:** Richmond city ~47% Black, ~39% white, ~7% Hispanic/Latino, diverse historically Black cultural heritage; Confederacy capital history intertwined with Black Richmond history; vibrant Black cultural institutions
- **Healthcare:** VCU Health (major academic medical system; Level 1 trauma center; substantial research); HCA Virginia (11,200 employees); Bon Secours Richmond (8,500 employees)
- Nationally recognized arts and culinary scene; James River outdoor culture

### 8. Political Stability & Trajectory
- Richmond IS Virginia's state capital — analogous governance dynamic to Sacramento: city and state government are co-located; state employees are a major constituency
- Under Youngkin (R, 2022–Jan 2026), city operated in friction on some issues; legislative alignment was buffered by Democratic-controlled General Assembly
- Virginia's 2025 elections: critical — Lt. Gov. candidate Abigail Spanberger (D) or Republican challenger; outcome shapes city-state alignment
- Avula is new (Jan 2025); one year of data insufficient to assess stability
- Water crisis (January 2025): blizzard exposed infrastructure failure; state (VDH) cited Richmond; corrective action ordered; raises concern about aging infrastructure
- Key long-term risks: state political trajectory (moderate-risk, competitive state); aging infrastructure (water, sewer); flood exposure along James River; gentrification tensions in historically Black neighborhoods

## WEATHER

### 1. Climate Overview
- **Köppen:** Cfa — humid subtropical, no dry season
- Latitude 37.54°N, elevation ~167 ft; Piedmont region between Blue Ridge to the west and Coastal Plain to the east
- Annual mean: 58°F (14.5°C); January mean 38°F → July mean ~80.5°F
- Annual precipitation: 44.1 inches, distributed evenly throughout year (no dry season)
- Annual snowfall: 8.8 inches (1991–2020 average, declining trend from 10.3" in prior 30-year period)
- Annual sunshine: **2,696 hours/year**

### 2. Winter
- January avg high: 47°F / low: 30°F; mean 38°F; ~21 freezing days in January
- Record low: historically in the -2°F to 0°F range
- Annual snowfall: 8.8" average; January is snowiest month (avg 4.5")
- **Primary hazard: freezing rain and ice storms**, not heavy snow
- January 5-6, 2025: winter storm triggered water treatment plant failure (5-day boil water advisory)
- January 23-24, 2026: "catastrophic" ice storm; governor urged residents off roads; significant power outages
- Ice infrastructure challenge: Mid-Atlantic cities underprepared for sustained ice; treatment and road-clearing capacity is limited
- Primary cold risks for older residents: falls on ice, hypothermia in power outages, cardiovascular stress during ice-storm events
- Freezing rain > snow as the character of Richmond winter

### 3. Summer
- July avg high: 90.9°F / low: 70.2°F; heat index: **107.6°F**; humidity 68%
- August avg high: 88.3°F / low: 68.5°F; heat index: 102.2°F; humidity 71%
- 43 days/year at or above 90°F
- Overnight lows stay in the 70s — insufficient for recovery; urban heat island effect real
- **Humid heat** — physiologically more challenging than dry heat of same temperature; wet-bulb stress higher
- AC universal; housing stock prepared
- No equivalent of Sacramento's Delta Breeze — no reliable afternoon cooling mechanism
- Heat illness risk concentrated in multi-day events with high overnight lows; significant risk for cardiovascular patients

### 4. Sunshine & SAD Risk
- 2,696 annual sunshine hours (above U.S. average ~2,500)
- December: avg 5.1 sunshine hours/day (shortest month)
- Latitude 37.5°N: daylight length adequate (10 hrs 30 min at December solstice)
- Winter light deprivation: frontal system cloud cover in winter, not marine overcast; periods of gray but no chronic persistent overcast like Seattle
- SAD risk: moderate — lower than Seattle or Portland; higher than San Diego or Honolulu; comparable to mid-Atlantic baseline
- Winter light is episodically interrupted by storm systems; brighter than most Pacific Northwest locations

### 5. Extreme Weather & Natural Hazards
- **Flood: primary hazard.** James River flooding from tropical systems and snowmelt; Hurricane Gaston (2004) killed 9 people in Richmond, condemned 20 blocks of Shockoe Bottom (10-foot floodwater); Tropical Storm Agnes (1972) shut downtown for days; regular tropical system impacts mid-August through mid-October
- 14% of city buildings at some flood risk (First Street Foundation)
- High-risk zones: Shockoe Bottom, Manchester, low-lying James River areas
- **Hurricanes:** Inland Richmond receives primarily flood risk, not wind risk; tropical storms routinely deliver multi-inch rainfall events
- **Ice storms:** Documented recurring hazard; January 2025 and January 2026 both notable; causes power outages, falls, structural damage
- **Tornadoes:** Occasional in Virginia; not a primary Richmond risk comparable to Midwest
- **Earthquake:** Low risk; not on major fault

### 6. Air Quality
- **2025 ALA "State of the Air":**
  - Ozone: Grade B; 165th worst in nation (improving — 4th consecutive year at "best ever" ozone performance)
  - Particle pollution (PM2.5): Worsening — daily measure dropped from B to C grade; 98th worst for short-term particle pollution (vs 116th worst prior year); driven by 2023 wildfire smoke impacts
- Summer ozone season: primary concern June–September
- Winter: indoor wood burning + vehicle emissions; inversions during cold snaps degrade PM2.5
- Wildfire smoke: growing regional threat; Northeast corridor fires and mid-Atlantic fires increasing frequency
- Air quality: moderate concern, better than Sacramento but worse than San Diego

### 7. Water Supply
- **Source:** James River exclusively — dam at Williams Island diverts to Byrd Park settling basins → treatment plant
- Treatment plants built 1924 and 1950; capacity 132M GPD; typical daily use ~45M GPD
- **January 2025 water crisis:** blizzard-triggered power failure at treatment plant; 5-day boil water advisory across Richmond and Henrico; completely avoidable per VDH/EPA investigations
- Root cause: "winter mode" operation created single point of failure; deferred maintenance; faulty backup generators; no redundancy
- VDH cited Richmond and demanded corrective action; EPA audit identified unaddressed infrastructure issues
- Regional drought resilience: Cobb's Creek Reservoir (Cumberland County, 14.8B gallon capacity) began filling May 2024 — off-stream storage for dry years
- System serves Henrico and Chesterfield counties as well — regional interconnection
- Aging infrastructure is a documented, formally cited concern — not speculative

### 8. How Residents Adapt
- Summer: AC is universal; outdoor activity structured around early morning and evening; farmers markets and outdoor dining concentrate in May, September, October
- James River culture: residents use the James River parks, Belle Isle, pipeline walk, and Oregon Hill overlooks for outdoor recreation — the river is an active lifestyle anchor despite flood risk
- Ice storm protocol: stock up before storms; stay home; city sands/plows but capacity limited for severe icing
- Flood awareness: residents in low-lying areas (Shockoe Bottom, Manchester) increasingly aware of flood vulnerability; some self-select against those neighborhoods
- Wildfire smoke: emerging adaptation — HEPA purifiers, air quality app monitoring during smoke events; less developed than Sacramento protocols

### 9. Climate Change Trajectory
- **Heat:** 7 days/year over 95°F (1990 baseline) → projected ~39 days/year by 2050
- **Precipitation:** 44.2" → projected 48.1" by 2050; 100-year storm to be 14% more intense
- **Flooding:** Worsening; more intense atmospheric moisture loading will amplify James River flood peaks; ~3,800 sites in region projected to flood by 2050; toxic industrial sites along James threatened by combined river rise and sea level rise
- **Sea level:** Richmond is 100+ miles inland; direct sea level impact minimal; Hampton Roads (downstream) projects +15–18 inches by 2050; saltwater intrusion into James River estuary will intensify
- **Urban heat island:** Historically redlined Richmond neighborhoods already documented as hotter than tree-canopied areas; heat island effects compound projected temperature increase
- City maintains RVAgreen 2050 climate action plan; moderate commitment level

### 10. Comparisons to Baselines
- **vs San Diego:** Richmond summers are far hotter and more humid (107°F heat index vs San Diego's ~76°F July avg high, low humidity); Richmond winters are colder with ice/snow risk vs San Diego's mild 66°F January highs; Richmond has recurring flood and ice hazards vs San Diego's minimal hazard profile; annual sunshine is moderately lower (2,696 vs ~3,200 hours); Richmond's hurricane/tropical flooding adds a seasonal hazard with no San Diego equivalent
- **vs Seattle:** Richmond has more annual sunshine (2,696 vs ~2,170 hours); Richmond summers are hot and humid (107°F heat index) vs Seattle's mild 75°F July; Richmond winters are colder and ice-prone vs Seattle's mild maritime winter; both have moderate flood exposure; Richmond lacks Seattle's chronic marine overcast but has more extreme seasonal swings
- **vs Columbia MO:** Very similar climate character — both humid subtropical, both hot humid summers, both ice/snow winters with limited infrastructure; Richmond's July heat index (107.6°F) slightly exceeds Columbia's (~100–104°F avg); Richmond has additional hurricane/tropical flooding risk absent from Columbia; Columbia has greater tornado exposure; Richmond snowfall (8.8") is less than Columbia's (~14"); climates are closely comparable
- **vs Richland WA:** Richland has dry hot summers (~94°F July high but low humidity, no heat index amplification); Richmond's 90.9°F July high with 68% humidity produces dramatically worse felt conditions; Richland winters are cold, dry, bright (-below freezing January nights); Richmond winters are milder but wetter, cloudier, and ice-prone; Richland gets ~8" annual precip; Richmond gets 44"; the trade is dry/bright/cold vs humid/mild/gray winters, and dry/tolerable vs humid/oppressive summers
