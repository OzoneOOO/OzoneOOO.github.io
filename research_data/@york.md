# York, PA — Research Notes
**Date gathered:** 2026-04-25  
**Coordinates:** 39.96°N, 76.73°W  
**Note:** This is York City (pop. ~44,000), distinct from York County (pop. ~460,000). The city is a small, post-industrial urban core surrounded by a much larger, more affluent, more conservative county. Data below distinguishes city vs. county where relevant.

---

## POLITICS DIMENSIONS

### 1. Governance Form
- Mayor-council charter under Pennsylvania's Optional Third Class Charter Law (in effect since January 1, 1962)
- Mayor serves as chief executive; City Council holds legislative authority and authorizes expenditures
- Current mayor: Michael Helfrich
- City Council: 7 members
- York is the county seat of York County

### 2. Fiscal Health
- York City has a history of significant fiscal stress; city was a designated Act 47 distressed municipality under Pennsylvania's Municipalities Financial Recovery Act; distressed designation was rescinded around 2013 after implementation of a recovery plan
- City's tax base fell approximately 17% during fiscal distress period; residents bore a tax burden increase of ~60%
- Household income disparity between the City of York and surrounding townships: ~57%
- Pennsylvania state-level context: Commonwealth collected $321M more than estimated in FY2024–25; sitting on nearly $11B budget surplus; received two credit rating upgrades (Fitch upgraded PA from AA- to AA in 2023)
- City's financial recovery remains constrained by a shrinking and lower-income urban tax base relative to surrounding suburban municipalities
- York County's finances are separate and considerably more stable than the city's

### 3. Retiree Tax Burden
- Pennsylvania flat income tax: **3.07%** on taxable income — one of the simplest state income tax systems
- **Pennsylvania exempts ALL retirement income** from state tax: Social Security, pension payments, 401(k)/IRA distributions — all excluded from PA taxable income; among the most retiree-friendly states in the nation
- Pennsylvania has **no state tax on retirement income** of any kind
- **York City Earned Income Tax (EIT):** residents pay a local EIT in addition to state income tax; York City rate is among the higher local EITs in the region (exact current rate: verify via York Adams Tax Bureau)
- **Property tax:** York County effective rate ~1.52%; median annual property tax in York County ~$2,674 on a median home value of ~$175,500 — moderate nationally, among lower in Pennsylvania
- Pennsylvania has an inheritance tax (4.5% for lineal heirs, 12% for siblings, 15% for others) — unique among Mid-Atlantic states
- Senior property tax relief available through Pennsylvania's Property Tax/Rent Rebate Program

### 4. Crime
- **Violent crime rate (2024): 710 per 100,000** — 92% above the U.S. national average
- **Property crime rate (2024): 2,559 per 100,000** — 31% above the U.S. national average
- York is safer than approximately **0% of Pennsylvania cities** and 9% of all U.S. cities
- 2024 homicides: 5 (down 2 from 2023); overall crime fell 12% from 2023 to 2024
- Individual risk: 1-in-189 chance of violent victimization; 1-in-54 for property crime; 1-in-42 overall
- Crime is heavily concentrated in the city proper; York County's suburban and rural areas have substantially lower crime rates
- York City crime rates reflect challenges common to post-industrial small cities: concentrated poverty, high income disparity, and limited economic opportunity

### 5. Housing Policy & Market
- **City of York median home price:** ~$171K (October 2025, up 6.6% YoY) — among the most affordable in Pennsylvania
- **York County median home price:** $294,900 (2025, 12th consecutive record high year)
- **3-bedroom rental:** ~$1,796/month countywide
- Median list price April 2025: ~$285,000; median price per square foot ~$157
- Market is competitive; peak selling season shifted to fall 2025
- Housing affordability is a relative strength; prices well below state urban averages (Philadelphia, Pittsburgh)
- City lacks a major ongoing housing redevelopment initiative comparable to larger cities; some historic district preservation activity downtown

### 6. Economic Trajectory
- York's economy is **manufacturing-dominant**: 21,645 manufacturing jobs; one of the top manufacturing employment centers in Pennsylvania
- Major employers: UPMC Pinnacle Memorial (healthcare), Johnson Controls (industrial), United Natural Foods, Weis Markets, Pennsylvania state government, York College of Pennsylvania, BAE Systems, Frito-Lay
- York County Economic Alliance promotes industrial and light-manufacturing recruitment
- Limited technology or knowledge-economy presence; economy structurally dependent on manufacturing and healthcare
- Unemployment rate: comparable to Pennsylvania average (state ~3–4%); specific current figure not available but York MSA tracks state trends
- York County median household income ~$63K (city significantly lower, below $40K); income disparity between city and county is among the highest in PA
- York's economy has stabilized from post-industrial decline but has not transitioned to a high-growth knowledge economy

### 7. Public Transit & Walkability
- **City-wide Walk Score:** not definitively published but characterized as "somewhat walkable"
- **Downtown York Walk Score: ~90** ("walker's paradise" in downtown core)
- Most walkable neighborhoods: Downtown, West Philadelphia Street, Historic Newton Square
- **Transit:** ~14 bus lines serving York via rabbittransit (York County Public Transportation Authority); described as "some transit" — not robust for car-free living across the city
- **Bikeability:** "somewhat bikeable"; no platinum cycling designation; infrastructure less developed than comparable progressive cities
- Car dependency is the norm outside downtown; metro area is predominantly suburban and rural
- York is not a Walkable Communities gold/silver/bronze designee at national level

### 8. LGBTQ+ Environment & Social Climate
- HRC Municipal Equality Index: **York, PA is not in the MEI rated cities list** for 2025 (city is below the population threshold and not selected by HRC state groups); no formal MEI score available
- York County votes Republican in nearly all elections; county political culture is socially conservative
- Pennsylvania state law does not include explicit statewide LGBTQ nondiscrimination protections in all areas
- City of York has a more mixed political character than the county, with some progressive urban voters, but no documented LGBTQ-specific city ordinances or infrastructure comparable to larger Pennsylvania cities
- No notable LGBTQ district, cultural infrastructure, or Pride organization at scale
- Philadelphia (90 miles east) and Baltimore (50 miles south) provide regional LGBTQ infrastructure for residents who travel

### 9. Political Stability (20–30 Year Horizon)
- **York County** is a reliably Republican county; GOP margins are consistent and wide; county has voted Republican in presidential elections by substantial margins for decades; not a political battleground (races come within 5 points less than 1% of the time)
- **York City** itself is more mixed, with urban Democratic voting patterns typical of small PA cities, but is embedded in and governed by state and county structures that are Republican-dominated
- Republican-controlled Pennsylvania state legislature; Democratic Gov. Josh Shapiro elected 2022 provides some executive balance
- Pennsylvania is a presidential battleground state at the statewide level, but York County is not competitive
- State preemption of local policies is a risk (Pennsylvania has preempted local minimum wage ordinances); city's policy autonomy constrained by state
- Long-term trajectory: York County demographic trends do not suggest a shift toward competitive politics within a 20–30 year horizon; city will remain embedded in a conservative county and state legislative environment
- No geopolitical instability concerns; stable mid-Atlantic location

- **Köppen classification: Cfa** (humid subtropical) — hot, humid summers; cool-to-cold winters; no dry season; precipitation year-round
- Located in south-central Pennsylvania, in the Piedmont region; elevation ~400 feet
- Codorus Creek flows through city center; Susquehanna River ~15 miles west
- No significant marine moderation; inland continental patterns dominate

### Precipitation
- Annual precipitation: ~45 inches — above U.S. average
- Annual snowfall: ~27 inches — moderate; occasional significant snowstorms
- September is typically wettest month; precipitation distributed year-round
- Summer convective thunderstorms common; significant rainfall events frequent

### Extreme Weather
- **Flooding: HIGH RISK** — Codorus Creek runs through city center; York County is in the Susquehanna River Basin, described as "one of the nation's most flood-prone areas"
- Susquehanna mainstem has flooded 14 times since 1810 (~every 15 years); averages $150M in annual flood damage across the basin
- Hurricane Agnes (1972): 13.5 inches of rain in 24 hours in York; Codorus Creek split the city in two
- Flash flooding at multiple downtown intersections; ice jam flooding is an additional winter hazard
- **Tornadoes:** lower frequency than Midwest but some risk; mid-Atlantic region subject to occasional tornadoes from severe thunderstorms
- **Winter storms:** ice storms and heavy snow possible; Arctic outbreaks occur but less frequent and severe than more northern cities
- **Heat:** York has the **highest projected heat risk among Pennsylvania cities studied**; currently ~12 days/year over 90°F; projected to reach 30+ days by 2050

### Air Quality
- AQI: moderate range; IQAir data shows typical AQI around 50–70 ("Moderate" category)
- Pennsylvania has industrial air quality concerns in some regions; York County has manufacturing concentration
- PM2.5 is the primary concern; occasional unhealthy days more common than in cleaner-air cities
- American Lung Association grades Pennsylvania cities variably; York area is not among the cleanest

### Water Access & Quality
- **Water supply:** York Water Company draws from Codorus Creek (south and east branches); two reservoirs — Lake Williams and Lake Redman — hold up to 2.23 billion gallons; Susquehanna River pumping station (15-mile pipeline) supplements during drought at up to 12 million gallons/day
- Codorus Creek flows through city center but is a small, channelized urban creek — limited recreational water access
- **No major lake, bay, or ocean access** within the city or immediate metro area
- Susquehanna River is ~15 miles west but does not provide waterfront amenity for city residents
- Water quality: treated drinking water meets all federal/state standards
- Recreational water quality of Codorus Creek historically degraded; agricultural and urban runoff are ongoing concerns in the broader watershed
- No meaningful daily water access comparable to coastal or lake cities

### Climate Change Projections
- Pennsylvania projected to warm **5.9–7°F** by 2050
- **York has the highest heat risk among Pennsylvania cities** studied: 12 days/year over 90°F currently → projected 30+ days by 2050; hot nights increasing
- Intense precipitation events projected to increase by ~8% by 2050; flood risk worsens
- Susquehanna watershed flood risk already severe; climate change amplifies storm runoff and extreme rainfall events
- Wildfire risk: low direct risk; some smoke transport from regional fires possible
- Pennsylvania generally: longer hot seasons, stronger storms, more intense rainfall events, reduced winter cold but not elimination of winter
