## POLITICS

### 1. Governance & Electoral Structure
- Council-manager form; "weak mayor" — City Manager appointed by mayor, serves at council's pleasure
- Mayor: **Kevin McCarty** (D), former Assemblymember District 6, sworn in Dec 2024 replacing Darrell Steinberg (2016–2024)
- 8-member district council: Lisa Kaplan, Roger Dickinson, Karina Talamantes, Phil Pluckebaum, Caity Maple, Eric Guerra, Rick Jennings II, Mai Vang — all elected 2024
- Next city council elections: primary June 2, 2026; general November 3, 2026
- Sacramento IS the state capital — city/state political alignment high; no state-preemption tension

### 2. Fiscal Health & Tax Structure
- **FY2025/26 budget:** $1.7B total; closed $62.2M structural deficit without layoffs
  - Solutions: $34.6M ongoing + $35.2M one-time
  - Structural cause: operating expenses growing faster than revenues (not economic downturn)
- **FY2026/27:** projected gap not yet quantified; City Manager budget due end of April 2026
- **CA retiree tax burden:**
  - Social Security: NOT taxed by California
  - Pensions/IRAs/401k: fully taxed as ordinary income at CA progressive rates (1%–13.3%)
  - Property tax: Prop 13 caps base at 1% assessed value, max 2%/year increase
  - Property Tax Postponement Program available for qualifying seniors
  - Senior credit up to $1,860 if income under ~$98,652
  - No local earnings tax

### 3. Public Safety
- 2025 overall crime down 12% vs 2024 (all seven tracked categories)
- Violent crime down 10.4%: 3,925 → 3,518 incidents
- Homicides: 54 (2024 data) = 10 per 100,000
- Violent crime rate: 902 per 100,000 — bottom 10% of all U.S. cities; 75% above national average
- Recent trend is strongly positive; starting point remains elevated

### 4. Housing & Development
- Median home price: ~$500–510K (March 2026), down ~1.2% YoY
- Median rent 1–2BR: ~$2,200/month
- Sellers' market; tight inventory; Prop 13 locks in existing owners
- Forecast: 3–5% annual appreciation through 2026
- Mechanism: geography (valley constraints + Sierra/Coast Range), demand from state workers and in-migration

### 5. Economic Trajectory
- State capital drives large government employment base (state agencies, legislature)
- Diversifying into: healthcare, technology, logistics, education
- Metro job growth: 3.1% over past year — nearly triple CA statewide pace
- Total 4-county metro employment: ~1,107,500 (Dec 2025)
- Population growth trend: positive; fueling construction, retail, services
- UC Davis (Davis, ~15 min) is major anchor for research, healthcare, talent

### 6. Transit & Infrastructure
- **SacRT light rail:** 42.9 miles, 3 lines, 53 stations, 121 vehicles; 22,100 weekday boardings (Q4 2025); 15th busiest in US
- New Siemens S700 low-floor trains entered service Sept 2024
- 5-year modernization project underway 2023–2027
- **Bus:** 76 routes, 5am–11pm daily; buses act as light rail feeders
- **Car-free realistic:** Midtown and Downtown only; broader metro is car-dependent
- **Airport:** Sacramento International (SMF) — direct flights to major domestic hubs; limited international

### 7. Social Environment
- California ranks among most comprehensive LGBTQ+ legal protection states
- Sacramento MEI score: not retrieved directly — search to HRC MEI CA page for exact figure
- UC Davis Medical Center: major academic medical system; UCDMC is Level 1 trauma center
- Racially diverse metro; large Southeast Asian and Latino communities
- Progressive city politics in a progressive state — no internal city/state conflict on social issues

### 8. Political Stability & Trajectory
- Sacramento is the seat of state government — uniquely insulated from state preemption conflicts
- New mayor (McCarty) and new council (2024) — insufficient data to assess coalition durability
- City council elections June/November 2026 — first major test for McCarty's agenda
- Biggest 10–20 year risk: structural budget stress if state revenues decline (city revenues tied to state economy); wildfire/flood fiscal shock; climate migration pressures
- Democratic supermajority in Sacramento and statewide — social environment stable over projected horizon

## WEATHER

### 1. Climate Overview
- **Köppen:** Csa — hot-summer Mediterranean (dry-summer subtropical)
- Latitude 38.6°N, elevation ~30 ft; Central Valley floor, Sierra Nevada to east, Coast Ranges to west
- Annual mean: 62.2°F; range Jan avg 47°F → Jul avg 77°F
- Annual precipitation: 24.1 inches (mostly Oct–April; virtually none June–Sept)
- Annual sunshine: **3,495 hours/year** (~300 equivalent sunny days)

### 2. Winter
- January avg high: 56°F / low: 39°F
- Record low: 17°F (Dec 11, 1932); January record low: ~21°F (Jan 2007)
- Frost: ~4 events/year average; no meaningful snow
- **Defining winter feature: tule fog** — radiation fog Nov–March; can persist 16–21+ consecutive days; visibility <600 ft; in severe cases <10 ft
- Tule fog traps air pollutants; driving hazard; documented psychological wear on residents
- Primary cold-weather risk for elderly: hypothermia during fog inversions, not extreme cold events

### 3. Summer
- July avg high: 94.6°F / low: 59.2°F; heat index ~102°F; humidity 46%
- August avg high: 93.6°F / low: 59.5°F; heat index ~100°F; humidity 48%
- Dry heat — low relative humidity relative to Eastern US; nights cool significantly
- AC ownership near-universal; housing stock prepared
- Urban heat island effect present but moderated by cool delta breeze (afternoon "Delta Breeze")
- Days over 100°F: currently ~15/year

### 4. Sunshine & SAD Risk
- 3,495 annual sunshine hours; January: 6.22 hours/day average
- Tule fog is the primary winter light deprivation mechanism — not latitude
- Sacramento at 38.6°N has adequate winter daylight length; the fog, not day length, suppresses irradiance
- Extended fog events (weeks) can replicate marine overcast conditions despite clear-day potential
- SAD risk is real but episodic and fog-dependent, not structurally embedded like Seattle's marine overcast

### 5. Extreme Weather & Natural Hazards
- **Flood: Sacramento identified as the nation's greatest metropolitan flood risk**
  - American + Sacramento rivers; 20 federal disaster declarations since 1950
  - Major floods: 1986, 1995, 1997, 2006, 2017
  - 2022 Central Valley Flood Protection Board: "Catastrophic flooding will occur; the only question is when"
  - Levee system provides substantial but not absolute protection
  - 44% of city buildings at some flood risk per First Street Foundation
- **Earthquake:** Low-moderate risk; Sacramento suffered little damage from 1989 Loma Prieta (M7.1, 60 mi away)
- **Tornado, hurricane:** Not a meaningful risk
- **Wildfire:** City itself not at direct risk; regional smoke impact is significant (see Air Quality)

### 6. Air Quality
- **Ozone:** Sacramento-Roseville ranked among top 5 worst ozone-polluted metros in US (American Lung Association)
- Summer ground-level ozone is the primary hazard — formed by vehicle/industrial emissions in heat
- Valley geography traps pollutants; inversions worsen winter wood-smoke PM2.5
- Wildfire smoke: increasingly severe seasonal threat (summer/fall); regional fires push PM2.5 to Unhealthy levels
- PM2.5: improving trend; 2024 had no 24-hour national standard exceedances, but EPA tightened PM2.5 standards in 2024
- Air quality represents a genuine long-term concern for respiratory/cardiovascular patients

### 7. Water Supply
- Primary sources: American River (Folsom Lake reservoir) + Sacramento River + groundwater basin
- $55M state investment (2023) in regional water resilience — 21 projects for dry-year supply
- WaterFuture program: $200M conjunctive use program targeting 100,000 AF/year drought-resilient supply
- Water Bank: 90,000+ AF groundwater storage recoverable in dry years
- Risk: Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta saltwater intrusion as sea level rises; upstream snowpack decline
- Multi-source system with active investment — more resilient than single-source cities

### 8. How Residents Adapt
- AC standard in all housing — summer heat management well-established
- Outdoor life concentrated in spring (March–May) and fall (October–November) — optimal seasons
- Fog season (Dec–Feb): residents stay home more, tolerate reduced visibility and light
- Delta Breeze: afternoon wind off San Francisco Bay reliably cools summer evenings — locals time outdoor activities around it
- Wildfire smoke events: HEPA air purifiers common; N95 masking during bad smoke days
- Farmers markets and outdoor dining culture thrives outside the fog and smoke windows

### 9. Climate Change Trajectory
- **Heat days over 102°F:** projected to reach ~30/year by 2050 (up from current ~15); up to 31 under high-emissions scenario
- **Flooding:** climate-intensified atmospheric rivers will worsen already high flood risk; "catastrophic flooding is when, not if"
- **Delta intrusion:** sea level rise pushes saltwater further into Delta, threatening freshwater supply and agriculture
- **Fog decline:** tule fog has been declining (Climate Central data) due to warming — reduces a known nuisance but may reduce agricultural chill-hour accumulation
- City has an active Climate Action and Adaptation Plan but the structural flood exposure is not eliminable by local policy

### 10. Comparisons to Baselines
- **vs San Diego (ideal baseline):** Sacramento's winters are foggier and colder; summers are hotter (94°F vs 76°F avg July high) but comparably dry. Annual sunshine similar in count but fog-interrupted in winter. San Diego has no meaningful extreme weather; Sacramento has serious flood risk and ozone.
- **vs Seattle (dark baseline):** Sacramento has dramatically more annual sunshine (3,495 vs ~2,170 hours). Extended tule fog events can temporarily replicate Seattle's overcast suppression. Summer heat far exceeds Seattle's mild 75°F July average. No comparable marine cloud deck.
- **vs Columbia MO (continental baseline):** Sacramento eliminates Missouri's tornadoes, ice storms, and humid summers. Summer heat is comparable in intensity (94°F vs 89°F) but Sacramento's is dry. Sacramento's flood risk is arguably worse than Columbia's storm risk in absolute terms.
- **vs Richland WA (arid baseline):** Very similar hot-dry summer profile (both ~94°F July highs). Sacramento winters are milder (56°F vs 37°F Jan high) and wetter (24" vs 8" annual). Richland gets real snow; Sacramento gets fog. Richland has bright, cold, clear winters; Sacramento has dim, mild, fogged winters.
