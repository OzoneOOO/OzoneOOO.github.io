Madison's climate is classified Köppen Dfa — humid continental with hot summers — sitting near the Dfb boundary. The defining feature is four highly distinct seasons separated by sharp transitions, with severe winters that regularly drop below 0°F and warm, humid summers. There is no dry season; precipitation is distributed across the calendar year with a slight summer maximum.

The city sits on a narrow isthmus between Lake Mendota and Lake Monona, which produces local lake-effect microclimate moderation but does not significantly buffer the deep continental cold of mid-winter. Annual temperature swings are wide by U.S. standards, with monthly mean highs ranging from the mid-20s°F in January to roughly 79°F in July.

Summer is warm and humid. July is the warmest month, with average highs near 79°F and lows around 61°F; August follows closely at 77°F / 59°F, and June averages 75°F / 57°F. These values are slightly below the U.S. summer-high average for the same latitude band but feel warmer due to dewpoints typical of an interior continental location with abundant lake-driven moisture.

Extreme heat days above 90°F are historically uncommon (around 7 per year in the late 20th century), though projections show this rising substantially in coming decades. Overnight cooling is generally adequate, with summer lows commonly in the upper 50s to low 60s°F, allowing reliable nighttime temperature recovery — an important threshold for heat-health considerations.

Winters are severe by U.S. standards. January averages a high of 24°F and a low of 11°F; February averages 28°F / 14°F, and December 28°F / 16°F. Sub-zero overnight temperatures are routine in the heart of winter, and Arctic air outbreaks regularly drive readings well below 0°F. January 2026 produced the coldest air locally observed in several years.

The cold season is long: meaningful freezing temperatures persist from late November through March, and the final spring freeze can occur into April. Winter cold of this depth — sustained subfreezing daytime highs across multiple months — places Madison at the cold end of the spectrum for major U.S. metropolitan areas.

Annual precipitation totals approximately 37 inches (936 mm), close to the U.S. national average of about 38 inches. May is the wettest month and February the driest, with the warm-season peak driven primarily by convective thunderstorm activity. Precipitation is well-distributed across the year, consistent with a humid continental classification.

Annual snowfall typically ranges from 40 to 45 inches, well above the U.S. average of approximately 28 inches. Snow cover is common from December through February and contributes to the cold winter feel by sustaining low surface albedo-driven heat loss. Extended periods of continuous snow cover are normal rather than exceptional.

Annual sunshine totals approximately 2,773 hours, slightly above the U.S. average of roughly 2,500 hours. The seasonal distribution is highly uneven: summer months exceed 9 hours of sunshine per day, with June and July averaging 10 hours/day, while December and January fall to 4.0 and 3.3 hours/day respectively. November averages just 4 hours/day.

The winter daily sunshine average of 3.3 hours in January is below the U.S. winter mean and approaches thresholds associated with seasonal light deficits documented in clinical literature. Combined with short photoperiod at 43°N latitude, the November–January window produces extended periods of low solar irradiance that are characteristic of interior northern-tier U.S. cities.

Tornado exposure is moderate by Midwest standards, averaging approximately 0.7 tornado days per year. A historically notable F1 tornado directly struck western Madison during a regional outbreak. Severe thunderstorms with damaging winds and hail are the more common warm-season hazard.

Winter storms are the dominant cold-season hazard, with regular Arctic outbreaks and occasional major snow events. Flooding affects roughly 16% of Madison buildings according to risk modeling, and several downtown intersections have a documented history of flash flooding during intense rainfall. Wildfire risk is rated relatively low — approximately 81% of buildings carry some modeled wildfire exposure, but the dominant pathway is regional smoke transport rather than direct ignition.

Air quality is consistently good. The annual average AQI sits in the 37–40 range, well within the EPA "Good" category (0–50). In 2025 the city recorded 130 good days — about 72% of monitored days — with only one day classified as Unhealthy. Madison receives a Grade A air quality rating and ranks better than approximately 60% of U.S. cities on composite air-quality metrics.

The principal pollutants of concern are PM2.5 from vehicle traffic and periodic wildfire smoke transported from regional and continental sources. Episodic smoke events have become more frequent in recent years but have not displaced the overall good baseline.

Madison sits on an isthmus between two large lakes — Lake Mendota (9,842 acres, 82 feet deep) and Lake Monona (3,274 acres, 64 feet deep) — with the broader Yahara chain (Wingra, Waubesa, Kegonsa) extending nearby. Direct daily water access is therefore unusually high among U.S. inland cities, with extensive shoreline parks, beaches, and recreational paths.

Drinking water is supplied from 22 wells through 30 reservoirs and 916 miles of pipe, and meets all federal and state standards. PFAS compounds have been detected at low (sub-regulatory) levels in roughly half of Madison's wells. Lake Monona shows elevated PFOS, and fish consumption advisories are in place for Lakes Monona, Waubesa, and Kegonsa. Recreational water quality has shown a measurable degradation trend: 2024 produced the highest algae bloom rate since monitoring began, and Mendota and Monona received "fair" ratings on phosphorus and clarity. Drinking water quality remains safe.

Mid-century projections show pronounced warming. Days above 90°F are expected to rise from approximately 7 per year (circa 1990) to about 38 per year by 2050 — a five-fold increase. Hot nights above 70°F are projected to roughly quadruple over the same horizon, eroding the historical pattern of reliable overnight summer cooling.

Annual precipitation is projected to increase modestly, from about 32.8 to 34.9 inches, with the share falling as intense downpours rising from 42% to 46%. Approximately 16% of buildings carry modeled flood risk under current conditions. The Dane County watershed has experienced drought in 33% of weeks since 2000, indicating notable hydrologic variability alongside the wetting trend.

Madison is sometimes discussed publicly as a potential "climate haven" given Great Lakes-region water availability and comparatively low drought and direct wildfire risk. The framing is contested: while heat, drought, and wildfire resilience are real relative advantages, the cold-and-dark winter profile, increasing summer heat, and rising flood-event intensity all complicate the picture.

Against the four baseline cities, Madison's winters are significantly colder than San Diego, Seattle, Columbia MO, and Richland WA. January daily sunshine of 3.3 hours is well below San Diego's roughly 7 hours, slightly below Richland's roughly 4 hours, broadly comparable to Columbia MO's roughly 4 hours, and slightly above Seattle's 2–3 hours. Annual sunshine of approximately 2,773 hours sits above Seattle (~2,170) and Columbia MO (~2,600), and below San Diego (~3,300) and Richland (~2,900).

Summer heat is broadly comparable to Columbia MO and somewhat milder than the peak-summer conditions experienced in Richland WA, while clearly warmer and more humid than San Diego or Seattle summers. Air quality is notably good year-round and competitive with all four baseline cities.

Daily water access is dramatically superior to Columbia MO and Richland WA, given Madison's two large lakes flanking the urban core. It does not match the open-ocean access of San Diego or the Puget Sound exposure of Seattle, but among inland U.S. cities the lake access is exceptional. The "climate haven" framing holds against the four baselines for heat, drought, and wildfire resilience, but does not hold for cold or winter darkness, where Madison is the most challenged of the comparison set.
